# Family Archives Guide

Family archives are often the heart of community memory.

They preserve everyday histories that may not appear in official records, including relationships, rituals, migration, labour, foodways, language, and emotional memory.

The Pulau Brani Community Archive recognises family archives as important forms of heritage stewardship.

---

# What counts as a family archive?

Family archives may include:

- photographs
- letters
- recipes
- prayer books
- cassette tapes
- maps
- certificates
- notebooks
- diaries
- household objects
- fishing tools
- textiles
- clothing
- kitchenware
- oral histories
- stories passed down verbally
- nicknames and family sayings
- recordings of songs, prayers, or pantun

Objects do not need to be “rare” or “museum-quality” to matter.

Everyday items often carry important cultural and emotional memory.

---

# Questions to ask when documenting materials

## Ownership and origin

- Who owned this item?
- Who kept it?
- Where did it come from?
- Is it connected to Pulau Brani or another island community?
- Is it connected to migration or resettlement?

## Memory and context

- What story is connected to this item?
- Why was it important?
- Was it used daily or only during special occasions?
- Does it connect to food, prayer, labour, festivals, or maritime life?
- Who would recognise or remember this?

## Consent and sensitivity

- Can this material be shown publicly?
- Are there family members who should be consulted?
- Does the material include private or sensitive information?
- Is the material spiritually or culturally sensitive?

---

# Photographing and documenting family objects

When documenting objects:

- Photograph from multiple angles
- Include close-ups of markings, handwriting, or texture
- Keep original captions or labels where possible
- Record dimensions if relevant
- Note materials used
- Record associated stories immediately

The story connected to the object is often as important as the object itself.

---

# Family photographs

When documenting photographs:

- Scan or photograph at high resolution
- Photograph both front and back
- Preserve handwritten notes
- Record names if known
- Record approximate dates if known
- Record locations if known
- Do not guess identities if uncertain

Suggested caption format:

`Description. People shown. Location. Approximate date. Contributor credit.`

---

# Oral explanation matters

Whenever possible, record family members explaining:

- who appears in photographs
- how objects were used
- family relationships
- migration histories
- festival practices
- food preparation methods
- sea routes or fishing knowledge

Short recorded explanations can preserve context that may otherwise disappear.

---

# Respect uncertainty

Not all memories will be complete or precise.

It is acceptable to record:

- “possibly”
- “approximately”
- “family believes”
- “uncertain”

The archive should preserve ambiguity honestly rather than invent certainty.

---

# AI and family archives

AI tools may help with:

- transcription
- translation
- metadata generation
- image organisation
- search

However:

- AI should not identify people automatically without review
- AI should not invent missing context
- AI should not override family interpretation
- Sensitive materials should not be uploaded to external systems without consent

---

# Long-term stewardship

Families may wish to:

- retain originals
- donate copies only
- restrict public access
- embargo materials temporarily
- allow educational use only

The archive should support flexible stewardship models.

---

# Final principle

Family archives are not only historical resources.

They are relationships, inheritances, emotional worlds, and living memory.
