## Project Overview

The Pulau Brani Project is a living archive documenting the histories, memories, traditions, and cultural life of Pulau Brani and Singapore’s island and maritime communities.

The project explores how memory, oral history, archives, technology, and community participation can help preserve and reawaken maritime heritage in contemporary Singapore.

The project approaches heritage as a living, evolving process rooted in community voices, kinship, migration, foodways, ritual, storytelling, and stewardship.

---

# Pulau Brani

Pulau Brani is one of Singapore’s southern islands, located near Keppel Harbour between mainland Singapore and Sentosa.

Historically, the island was home to vibrant kampong communities shaped by maritime life, fishing, labour, mobility between islands, religious practice, and strong social ties.

The name “Pulau Brani” is commonly understood to mean “Island of the Brave.”

Before modern redevelopment, Pulau Brani contained:

- kampongs
- football fields
- open-air cinemas
- jetties
- mosques and surau
- family homes
- shoreline gathering spaces
- maritime labour networks

Many former residents were resettled to mainland Singapore during redevelopment in the late 1960s and 1970s.

Despite resettlement, community memory and identity continue through oral histories, recipes, rituals, reunions, and intergenerational storytelling.

# Orang Pulau

“Orang Pulau” means “island people.”

In the context of Singapore and Pulau Brani, the term refers broadly to people and families connected to the southern islands and maritime settlements.

The term should not be understood as one single identity group.

Orang Pulau communities include diverse histories, ancestries, occupations, migration patterns, and lived experiences.

Island life was often deeply connected to:

- tides
- sea routes
- fishing
- mobility between islands
- trade
- kinship networks
- religious practice
- mutual support systems

---

# Orang Laut

“Orang Laut” means “sea people.”

Historically, Orang Laut communities across Southeast Asia played important roles in maritime navigation, fishing, trade routes, and coastal stewardship.

In the Singapore and Riau context, Orang Laut histories are connected to maritime movement, environmental knowledge, and long-standing sea-based ways of life.

The term “Orang Laut” includes many different communities and histories across the Malay world and should not be treated as a single uniform identity.

The Pulau Brani Project approaches Orang Laut heritage with care, nuance, and attention to community memory.

# Village Life

Former residents often describe kampong life on Pulau Brani as socially close-knit and community-oriented.

Memories include:

- children playing outdoors freely
- communal cooking
- neighbours sharing food
- festive gatherings
- fishing and swimming
- football matches
- island cinemas
- shoreline life
- open movement between homes

Community relationships were often intergenerational.

Knowledge and traditions were transmitted through everyday life rather than formal institutions.

---

# Foodways

Food played an important role in island identity, family memory, and communal life.

Examples of remembered food traditions include:

- seafood dishes
- sambal
- ikan preparations
- sotong dishes
- ulam
- kuih
- communal Hari Raya cooking
- kenduri food traditions

Cooking knowledge was often transmitted through families and community gatherings.

The Pulau Brani Project documents recipes not only as cuisine, but as forms of memory and cultural continuity.

---

# Rituals and Festivals

Community life included religious and cultural practices connected to:

- Ramadan
- Hari Raya
- kenduri gatherings
- weddings
- funerals
- communal prayer
- preparation rituals
- seasonal gatherings

Malam Tujuh Likur refers to the 27th night of Ramadan and is associated in many Malay communities with light, prayer, reflection, and preparation before Hari Raya.

Practices varied across families and communities.

# Oral Histories

The Pulau Brani Project places strong emphasis on oral history.

Oral histories preserve:

- lived memory
- family stories
- migration experiences
- food traditions
- maritime knowledge
- childhood memories
- ritual practices
- emotional relationships to place

The project recognises oral history as an important form of knowledge preservation.

---

# Storytelling and Folklore

The project documents not only historical events, but also folklore, myths, supernatural stories, and spiritual relationships connected to maritime spaces.

Some stories describe waterways as spiritually significant and connected to memory, danger, ancestors, or unseen presences.

These stories form part of broader Nusantara maritime cultural traditions.

---

# Resettlement and Transformation

Pulau Brani changed significantly during Singapore’s redevelopment and port expansion.

Many island residents were relocated to mainland housing estates.

Community memory often reflects both:

- pride in national development
- grief and displacement connected to the loss of island life

The project approaches these histories with care and complexity.

- oral history preservation

# Important Principles for Ask Pulau Brani

The Ask Pulau Brani chatbot must:

1. Use only approved and reviewed materials
2. Avoid speculation
3. Say “I don’t know” when information is uncertain
4. Respect community sensitivities
5. Preserve nuance
6. Avoid flattening diverse communities into one narrative
7. Encourage community contribution and correction
8. Prioritise dignity and stewardship over completeness
