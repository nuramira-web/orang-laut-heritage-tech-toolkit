# Ethical AI and Heritage

The Pulau Brani Project explores how AI may support heritage stewardship, accessibility, and intergenerational storytelling.

However, heritage is not simply data.

It involves:
- memory
- identity
- emotion
- spirituality
- language
- community relationships
- historical interpretation
- cultural nuance

AI systems must therefore be approached carefully.

---

# Key questions

Students and contributors are encouraged to ask:

- Who owns cultural memory?
- What should AI be allowed to interpret?
- What should remain human-led?
- What happens when AI misrepresents culture?
- Can archives ever be neutral?
- What is consent in the age of AI?
- What should not be digitised?
- How do we preserve nuance?
- How do we avoid flattening diverse communities?

---

# Risks

AI systems may:

- hallucinate
- oversimplify
- flatten dialect
- misread emotion
- erase uncertainty
- prioritise dominant narratives
- reproduce bias
- detach stories from context

---

# Principles

## 1. Community stewardship first

Communities should shape how their histories are documented, interpreted, and accessed.

## 2. Human review remains essential

AI-generated summaries, tags, relationships, and interpretations require review.

## 3. Consent matters

Not all materials should be used for AI training, retrieval, or public interfaces.

## 4. Preserve uncertainty

Archives should not present uncertain information as fact.

## 5. Context matters

Stories should remain connected to place, ritual, language, family, and lived experience.

## 6. Accessibility matters

AI may support:
- translation
- transcription
- search
- navigation
- intergenerational learning

when used responsibly.

---

# Final principle

The goal is not to automate heritage.

The goal is to cultivate more thoughtful, participatory, and ethical relationships to memory in the AI era.
