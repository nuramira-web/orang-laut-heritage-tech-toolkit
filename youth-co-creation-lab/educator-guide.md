# Educator Guide

The Youth Co-Creation Lab is designed for flexible use across:

- secondary schools
- junior colleges
- polytechnics
- universities
- museums
- community workshops
- interdisciplinary studios
- digital humanities programmes

The lab supports learning across:

- heritage studies
- AI literacy
- design
- storytelling
- history
- public policy
- media studies
- geography
- language
- digital humanities
- civic participation

---

# Suggested teaching approach

The lab works best when students:

- build things
- conduct interviews
- create interpretations
- collaborate across disciplines
- reflect critically on technology
- engage directly with community memory

The goal is not memorisation.

The goal is stewardship, interpretation, creativity, and ethical participation.

---

# Suggested session structure

## Session 1 — Introduction

Topics:
- Pulau Brani
- Orang Laut
- Orang Pulau
- oral history
- archives
- memory
- AI and heritage

---

## Session 2 — Community Memory

Students:
- explore oral histories
- examine photographs
- analyse rituals and foodways
- identify emotional themes

---

## Session 3 — AI and Interpretation

Students experiment with:
- transcription
- tagging
- archive search
- emotional mapping
- AI-assisted storytelling

Students discuss:
- consent
- hallucination
- authorship
- bias
- stewardship

---

## Session 4 — Creative Output

Students create:
- maps
- podcasts
- visual essays
- constellation graphs
- mini exhibitions
- digital stories
- archive interfaces

---

# Suggested reflection questions

- Who decides what becomes heritage?
- What is lost when memory disappears?
- Can AI preserve culture responsibly?
- What should never be automated?
- What does ethical stewardship look like?
- How do archives shape identity?
- What stories are missing from official history?
